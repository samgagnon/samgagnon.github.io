About Me

Samuel Gagnon-Hartman is a Ph.D. student in Physics at the Scuola Normale Superiore in Pisa, Italy. He is currently working on his thesis, "Multi-Messenger Probes of the Epoch of Reionization," under the supervision of Andrei Mesinger. His research involves developing methods for confirming a prospective detection of the 21-cm auto-correlation signal using other tracers of the Epoch of Reionization and Cosmic Dawn.

What does Danaan mean?

Danaan is the English rendering of Δαναοί, one of the most common demonyms used for the "Greeks" in the Illiad. Specifically, it means "the people of Danaus." Danaus was the mythological founder of Argos, and thus father of the Argives, another archaic demonym of the Greeks. What I find interesting about his myth is his supposed Egyptian origin: that he was the second son of the King of Egypt and fled along with his fifty daughters to Argos to escape the wrath of his brother, who had inherited the throne.

By coincidence, Danaan (as rendered in English) resembles Canaan, the promised land of the Israelites who also fled Egyptian persecution. Here we have two parallels, one of pure coincidence and another of some substance, both encoded in the same word. Not only can we extract a thread of common history, but also of common destiny: practically the whole of the Western canon rests upon the shoulders of the Greek and Hebrew traditions. What a treat then, that even in their earliest stages, when the Israelites and Greeks knew very little of one another, they already reflected each other in their mythologies, as if to foreshadow their shared destiny.

Danaan therefore takes on a special meaning in this website: it refers to a serendipitous clue, something seemingly innocuous which returns with force at the end of the age. We see it again and again in science, in literature, in history. Danaan was present when Leeuwenhoek first spied the microbe, when the Hobbits returned to the Shire only to find an unfamiliar land, and when Jacob and Esau made amends. Danaan reminds you to pay attention: you never know what's important to the plot!
